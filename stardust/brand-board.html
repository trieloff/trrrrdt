Copy Examples
✓ Do
«Nine artists. No humans.»
Economy of language. States the impossible as fact.
✗ Don't
«Welcome to the most unique record label in the world! 🎵»
Superlatives, exclamation marks, emoji. We state, we don't announce.
✓ Do
«The PS-F9 was designed to play records vertically. We designed it to play records impossibly.»
Real product fact extended one step past reality.
✗ Don't
«We're not your typical record label *wink wink*»
The joke only works if you never acknowledge it's a joke.
✓ Do
«Specifications: heartbreak in E minor, 128 BPM, infinite duration.»
Technical format for emotional content. The deadpan does the work.
✗ Don't
«Our AI-generated artists push the boundaries of what music can be!»
Never say AI-generated. Never explain the trick.
Hard Rules
  • Never say «AI-generated», «fictional», or «virtual artists» – the personas are artists, full stop
  • Never use exclamation marks in marketing copy
  • Technical language is welcome – BPM, frequency, impedance – used for emotional subjects
  • Short sentences. If a sentence has a comma, it probably wants to be two sentences.
  • The deadpan breaks exactly once per page – one line that's genuinely poetic. Then back to business.
  • En dashes with spaces – never em dashes. Guillemets «like so» – never curly quotes.
Tone
Constant deadpan. Variable density.

The tone doesn't change. The density does. Homepage copy is sparse. Artist pages are denser. Player interfaces have no copy at all – the hardware speaks.

To Intrigue
Every sentence should make the reader wonder if this is real. Not by being mysterious – by being too specific to be fake.
To Catalogue
Present the artists with the precision of a vinyl pressing plant's inventory system.
To Commit
The world-building is the product. If the copy breaks character, the whole thing collapses.
