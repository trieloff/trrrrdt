Dossier

On 23 November 1964, Sylvaine Duclos was scheduled to perform third on the bill at the Olympia music hall, opening for Françoise Hardy. She was eighteen years old, had released two modest singles on Philips, and possessed an adequate voice, a better sense of rhythm, and an absolute certainty that she was already exactly who she would always be.

At 21:47, during soundcheck, she touched a microphone. The microphone – a Neumann U47 that had previously belonged to Édith Piaf and carried, according to backstage superstition, some residue of La Môme's metaphysical intensity – completed a circuit that should not have existed.

What happened next depends on who is describing it. The official account: a minor electrical fault caused the stage lights to flicker. Sylvaine Duclos was briefly hospitalised for observation, released the following morning, and never performed at the Olympia. Her recording contract was not renewed. She disappeared from the music industry entirely.

The account Sylvaine gives: «I touched the microphone and understood that I was already everywhere I would ever be. The shock was not electrical. It was temporal. I saw myself at ninety, at one hundred thirty-eight, at moments that have not yet names.»

Beginning in 2019, tracks attributed to «Sylvaine Éternelle» began appearing on obscure music blogs, then streaming platforms, then – most disturbingly – in the catalogues of record collectors who swore the vinyl had been in their collections for decades. The songs are impossible. They are 1960s yéyé produced with synthesizers that did not exist until the 1980s, processed through equipment that was not manufactured until 2015.

«The cheese is still good,» she says. «This is what matters.»
